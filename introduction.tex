Symbolic execution (\SE) is a program analysis technique with applications in many areas,
including test generation, bug finding, and verification~\cite{CadarSen13,klee,exe,spf,bugredux,se-java-2004,se-numerical-2006,syminfer}.
Although \SE can perform verification~\cite{verifast,viper} via exhaustive path exploration,
unverified \SE engines (\eg \klee~\cite{klee}, Manticore~\cite{mossberg2019manticore}, ANGR~\cite{angr}) cannot provide formal safety guarantees.
%Indeed, as recently demonstrated by~\cite{klee-rocq},
Indeed, recent work by~\cite{klee-rocq} demonstrates the risk of excluding the \SE engine itself from the trusted computing base (TCB).
Obtaining a verified \SE engine that remains practical is highly challenging.
On the one hand,
verifying existing implementations requires immense effort,
as they typically consist of large codebases with complex third-party dependencies.
On the other hand,
verified implementations extracted from proof assistants~\cite{fse23wise,icfp22:keuchel} are restricted to simple programming languages,
and they lack advanced features such as search heuristics, constraint solving optimizations, and other performance optimizations.

Proof logging~\cite{lundberg2024proof,klee-rocq,de2008proofs,marx2013proof,flippo2024multi,mcilree2024proof,van2025efficient}
is an alternative approach inspired by \emph{translation validation}~\cite{samet,pnueli1998translation}.
In the context of \SE,
applying this approach means that the \SE engine generates a machine-checked safety proof tailored to each analyzed program.
This proof can be independently validated,
thus bypassing the need for a verified implementation.
Proof-logging \SE has been recently applied in the context of P4~\cite{lundberg2024proof} (a domain-specific language for network devices) and a subset of \llvm IR with integers~\cite{klee-rocq},
however, it has not yet been demonstrated on memory-manipulating programs.

% Our contribution
In this paper, we apply proof logging to memory-manipulating programs in \llvm IR.
To the best of our knowledge,
this is the first tool that generates machine-checked safety proofs for this subset of \llvm IR.
The high-level architecture of our approach, which builds on the framework of~\cite{klee-rocq}, is illustrated in~\Cref{fig:diagram}.
Our theoretical framework formalizes the \emph{mechanized} concrete and symbolic semantics,
where the latter specifies how instructions operate over symbolic values.
Based on the mechanized semantics,
our theoretical framework establishes a theorem stating that if the execution tree of the program satisfies certain safety properties then the program is safe with respect to the concrete semantics.
To leverage this theorem, %one must provide an execution tree that satisfies those properties,
the \SE engine is instrumented to generate a compliant execution tree.
The resulting proof thus comprises of two components:
a section containing general theory and a program-specific section generated by the \SE engine during the analysis.
Finally, the resulting proof is validated by the proof assistant.

% The challenge
Extending the approach in~\cite{klee-rocq} to support memory operations requires extending the theoretical framework and the instrumentation of the \SE engine,
a significant theoretical and engineering effort.
Extending the theoretical framework is challenging,
as it requires capturing the intricate specifications regarding data layouts, address spaces, dynamic allocations, pointers, and semantics of memory-related instructions.
To achieve this,
we first incorporate memory-related instructions into the mechanized concrete and symbolic semantics,
and then generalize the theoretical foundation to allow proving program safety based on properties of execution trees.
Extending the instrumentation of the \SE engine introduces additional challenges.
\SE engines use various memory-modeling optimizations that complicate proof generation.
For example, \klee uses multiple representations for memory objects and applies SMT encoding optimizations.
To bridge the gap between the mechanized symbolic semantics and the heavily-optimized behavior in the \SE engine, we introduce \emph{interface lemmas}.
These lemmas help to efficiently interact with various components of our framework,
while reducing the complexity of the resulting proofs.
Our theoretical framework is developed in \coq and comprises approximately 40,000 lines of code (LOC),
which more than doubles the 18,000 LOC framework of~\cite{klee-rocq}.
This substantial extension reflects the complexity of transitioning to a memory-aware framework.

The entire development of this paper comes with proofs in \coq~\cite{rocq}.

\begin{paragraph}{\textbf{Main Contributions:}}
\begin{itemize}
\item We developed a \coq framework consisting of a concrete and symbolic semantics for a subset of \llvm IR with memory operations and a theoretical foundation for constructing safety proofs.
\item We implemented an extension of \klee that generates safety proofs based on our framework.
\item We evaluated our approach on APIs from real-world benchmarks, and found previously unknown issues in the memory modeling in \klee.\footnote{
      These issues were confirmed by the maintainers of \klee. The link is omitted due to double-blind reviewing.
      }
\end{itemize}
\end{paragraph}

This paper is organized as follows.
In~\Cref{sec:overview}, we provide an overview of our approach.
In~\Cref{sec:background}, we provide background on \llvm IR and SMT.
In~\Cref{sec:concrete-semantics} and \Cref{sec:symbolic-semantics}, we present our concrete and symbolic semantics of a subset of \llvm IR with memory operations, respectively.
In~\Cref{sec:program-safety}, we present our theoretical foundation, which enables proving safety of prorgrams based on properties related to the symbolic semantics.
In~\Cref{sec:proof-generation}, we show how to instrument the \SE engine to generate safety proofs based on the theoretical foundation presented in~\Cref{sec:program-safety}.
In~\Cref{sec:implementation},
we provide details about our framework (in \coq) and our implementation (on top of \klee),
and describe the issues that we found in \klee.
In~\Cref{sec:evaluation}, we present our experimental results.
In~\Cref{sec:discussion}, we discuss the key aspects and implications of our work.
In~\Cref{sec:related}, we discuss related work, and in~\Cref{sec:conclusion}, we summarize the contributions of this paper and outline future work.
