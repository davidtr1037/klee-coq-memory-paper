Symbolic execution (\SE) is a program analysis technique with applications in many areas,
including test generation, bug finding, and verification~\cite{CadarSen13,klee,exe,spf,bugredux,se-java-2004,se-numerical-2006,syminfer}.
Although \SE has been used for verification tasks,
%one cannot trust unverified \SE engines to infer that a program is indeed safe,
unverified \SE engines cannot be trusted to guarantee program safety,
as recently demonstrated by~\cite{klee-rocq}.
However, obtaining a verified \SE engine that remains practical is highly challenging.
On the one hand,
verifying existing implementations requires immense effort,
as they typically consist of large codebases with complex third-party dependencies.
On the other hand,
verified implementations extracted from proof assistants~\cite{fse23wise,icfp22:keuchel} are restricted to simple programming languages,
and they lack advanced features such as search heuristics, constraint solving optimizations, and other performance optimizations.

Proof logging~\cite{lundberg2024proof,klee-rocq,de2008proofs,marx2013proof,flippo2024multi,mcilree2024proof,van2025efficient}
offers an alternative inspired by \emph{translation validation}~\cite{samet,pnueli1998translation}.
Rather than requiring a verified implementation,
the \SE engine generates a machine-checked safety proof tailored to each analyzed program.
The high-level architecture of proog logging is given in~\Cref{fig:diagram}.
A theoretical framework formalizes the concrete and symbolic semantics,
and provides a theorem stating that if the execution tree of the program satisfies certain properties then the program is safe with respect to the concrete semantics.
To apply this theorem, one must provide an execution tree that satisfies those properties,
and to do so, the \SE engine is instrumented to generate such an execution tree.
Hence, the final output is a proof consisting of two sections:
A section containing general theory and a program-specific section generated by the SE engine during the analysis.

Proof logging has been recently applied in the context of P4~\cite{lundberg2024proof} and a subset of \llvm with integers~\cite{klee-rocq},
however, it has not yet been demonstrated on memory-manipulating programs.
Memory modeling is one of the most intricate components of \SE engines,
%as well as in program analysis in general.
and integrating proof logging in this context is challenging.
To extend the approach in~\cite{klee-rocq} for memory operations,
one needs to formalize the memory model of \llvm and extend the \SE engine to generate safety proofs accordingly.
%Formalizing the memory model of \llvm is complex due to the detailed specifications related to
Such a formalization must capture the detailed specifications related to
data layouts, address spaces, dynamic allocations, pointers, and semantics of memory-related instructions.
Furthermore, \SE engines use various optimizations for memory modeling, which complicate proof generation.
For example, \klee~\cite{klee} uses multiple representations for memory objects and applies SMT encoding optimizations.

In this paper,
we apply proof logging to memory-manipulating programs in \llvm.
To the best of our knowledge,
this is the first tool that generates machine-checked safety proofs for this subset of \llvm IR.
Our approach builds on~\cite{klee-rocq} and introduces several substantial extensions,
requiring a significant theoretical and engineering effort.
First, we extend the concrete and symbolic semantics to support memory-related instructions.
Second, we extend the theoretical framework which allows to prove program safety based on properties of execution trees.
Lastly, we extend \klee to generate safety proofs based on our formalization.
To bridge the gap between the mechanized symbolic semantics and the heavily-optimized behavior in \klee,
we develop an additional theory that helps to interact with various parts of our formalization.
Our \coq formalization comprises approximately 40,000 lines of code (LOC),
which more than doubles the 18,000 LOC formalization of~\cite{klee-rocq}.
This substantial difference reflects the additional complexity of our formalization.

The entire development of this paper comes with proofs in \coq~\cite{rocq}.

\begin{paragraph}{\textbf{Main Contributions:}}
\begin{itemize}
\item We developed a framework in \coq consisting of a concrete and symbolic semantics for a subset of \llvm IR with memory operations and a theoretical foundation for constructing safety proofs.
\item We implemented an extension of \klee that generates safety proofs based on our framework.
\item We evaluated our approach on APIs from real-world benchmarks, and found previously unknown issues in the memory modeling in \klee.\footnote{
      These issues were confirmed by the maintainers of \klee. The link is omitted due to double-blind reviewing.
      }
\end{itemize}
\end{paragraph}

This paper is organized as follows.
In~\Cref{sec:background}, we provide background on \llvm IR and SMT.
In~\Cref{sec:overview}, we provide an overview of our approach.
In~\Cref{sec:concrete-semantics} and \Cref{sec:symbolic-semantics}, we present our concrete and symbolic semantics of a subset of \llvm IR with memory operations, respectively.
In~\Cref{sec:program-safety}, we present our theoretical foundation, which enables proving safety of prorgrams based on properties related to the symbolic semantics.
In~\Cref{sec:proof-generation}, we show how to instrument the \SE engine to generate safety proofs based on the theoretical foundation presented in~\Cref{sec:program-safety}.
In~\Cref{sec:implementation},
we provide details about our formalization (in \coq) and our implementation (on top of \klee),
and describe the issues that we found in \klee.
In~\Cref{sec:evaluation}, we present our experimental results.
In~\Cref{sec:discussion}, we discuss the key aspects and implications of our work.
In~\Cref{sec:related}, we discuss related work, and in~\Cref{sec:conclusion}, we summarize the contributions of this paper and outline future work.
